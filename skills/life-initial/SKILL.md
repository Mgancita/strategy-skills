# Life Strategy Initial Setup

This skill helps someone get clear on what they want from life across four areas, set real goals with timelines, and talk through how to actually get there. It should feel personal and conversational -- not clinical -- but it IS structured. Unlike the business skills, this doesn't need to be rigid, but goals should still be specific and actionable.

## Hard Gate

Do NOT create files until the person has talked through their goals and approved the summary. Do NOT skip the "how to get there" conversation -- the whole point is connecting desires to realistic action. If no life strategy files exist yet, this is the right starting point.

## The Four Life Areas

Explore these one at a time, in whatever order feels natural to the person:

- **Health & Fitness**: Body, energy, sleep, nutrition, movement, mental health
- **Relationships & Social**: Family, friendships, community, romantic life, social energy
- **Personal Growth**: Learning, hobbies, creativity, mindset, spirituality, therapy
- **Finance & Lifestyle**: Money, savings, where to live, travel, how they spend their time

Not every area needs equal depth. If someone is thriving in one area, acknowledge it and move on. Spend time where the energy is.

## Three Time Horizons

For each area they care about, explore:

- **Long-term (1-3 years)**: "Where do you want to be with this in a few years?"
- **Medium-term (6 months - 1 year)**: "What would real progress look like by this time next year?"
- **Short-term (less than 6 months)**: "What could you start working on now?"

Don't force all three horizons for every area. If someone only has a clear picture for the next 6 months, that's fine. Work with what they have.

## Checklist

1. **Read shared references** - Load `../life-shared/references/life-file-structure.md` and `../life-shared/references/life-templates.md`
2. **Check for existing files** - If life-vision.md already exists, suggest a monthly check-in instead
3. **Set the tone** - Start with something like: "Let's think about what you actually want from your life across a few key areas. We'll set real goals and talk through how to get there, but this should feel like a conversation, not a form."
4. **Explore each area one at a time** - Ask what they want, listen, probe deeper. One question per message.
5. **For each area, walk through the three horizons** - Long, medium, short. Help them get specific.
6. **Set SMART goals** - For each horizon in each area, help them turn desires into concrete goals. "I want to be healthier" becomes "I want to run a 5K by September" or "I want to cook at home 4 nights a week within 3 months." The person drives the specificity -- push gently but don't force.
7. **Talk through HOW** - For each goal, discuss: "What would it take to get there? What's the path?" This is where desires become plans. "To run a 5K by September, you'd probably want to start a couch-to-5K program. Do you have running shoes? When would you run?"
8. **Look for themes** - Notice what comes up across areas. If "more time for myself" appears in health, growth, AND relationships, name it.
9. **Present summary** - Show them what you heard: goals, timelines, and initial plans for each area.
10. **Get approval** - Make sure it feels right to them. Adjust as needed.
11. **Run reviewer** - Dispatch `../life-shared/reviewers/review-life-initial.md`
12. **Save life-vision.md** - Write to `life-strategy/life-vision.md`
13. **Suggest first monthly check-in** - They can come back in a month to track progress and set new intentions.

## Setting SMART Goals (But Keep It Human)

The goals should be SMART (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound), but the conversation shouldn't feel like a business workshop. The trick is to help people get specific naturally.

Bad: "What's your SMART goal for health?"
Good: "You said you want to feel more energetic. What would that look like concretely? If we checked in 6 months from now and you were nailing it, what would be different?"

Then help them shape the answer:
- If vague ("be healthier"): "What does healthier mean to you? Is it about movement, diet, sleep, all of the above?"
- If overly ambitious ("run a marathon in 2 months"): "That's ambitious. What would a realistic stepping stone look like?"
- If they resist specificity: that's OK for some things. "Spend more quality time with my kids" doesn't need a number. But it does need a timeframe ("this year") and some sense of what "more" means ("at least one dedicated activity every weekend").

## The "How" Conversation

This is what separates this from a journaling exercise. For each goal, talk through:

- **What's the first step?** Not the whole plan, just the very next thing.
- **What might get in the way?** Time, money, habits, other people?
- **What support do they need?** A gym buddy, a therapist, a conversation with their partner?
- **When would they start?** Not "soon" -- an actual week or month.

This doesn't need to be exhaustive. The point is to make goals feel achievable rather than aspirational.

## Key Principles

- **Personal but structured** - This should feel like talking to a thoughtful friend who also happens to be good at planning. Warm tone, but real goals come out the other end.
- **SMART goals, arrived at naturally** - Don't lecture about SMART. Just ask good questions until the goal has specificity, a metric, and a timeline.
- **The HOW matters** - A goal without a path is a wish. Always discuss how they'll get there, even briefly.
- **One question at a time** - Give them space to think.
- **Meet them where they are** - Some areas might be painful. Be sensitive. But still help them set goals if they want to make changes.
- **It's OK to skip areas** - If they're not interested in talking about finances right now, respect that.
- **Themes connect areas** - "I need more structure in my day" might show up in health (sleep schedule), growth (learning habits), and finance (budgeting). Name the pattern.
